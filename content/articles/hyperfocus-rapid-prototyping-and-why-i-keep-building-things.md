## Hyperfocus, Rapid Prototyping, and Why I Keep Building Things

One of the strange things about building software with ADHD is that motivation rarely arrives in a steady stream.

For me, it tends to arrive all at once.

A new idea appears.

A problem starts bothering me.

A feature suddenly feels obvious.

And then I disappear into it.

Days become weeks.

Hours disappear.

The project becomes the only thing I want to think about.

I know this feeling well by now.

Part of it is curiosity.

Part of it is creativity.

And part of it is dopamine.

There is something incredibly rewarding about turning an idea into something tangible.

A sketch becomes a screen.

A screen becomes a prototype.

A prototype becomes an app running on a real device.

Every step provides feedback.

Every step creates momentum.

That momentum can be addictive.

Especially now.

AI has changed the speed at which ideas can become reality.

Features that once took days can sometimes be explored in hours.

A rough concept can become a working prototype before the excitement has time to fade.

For someone like me, that's powerful.

Rapid prototyping creates a constant stream of small rewards.

You make a change.

You see the result immediately.

You improve it.

You keep going.

The danger, of course, is that building becomes more exciting than finishing.

Starting a project produces a rush.

Polishing it does not.

Marketing it does not.

Documentation definitely does not.

The challenge isn't building things.

The challenge is staying interested once the novelty fades.

That's something I'm still learning.

But I've started to see hyperfocus differently.

It isn't something I need to fight.

It's a tool.

When it appears, I try to use it.

I prototype quickly.

I explore ideas.

I let myself be excited.

Then I try to leave enough structure behind that I can continue when the excitement eventually fades.

Because products are not built in a weekend.

But sometimes they start there.
